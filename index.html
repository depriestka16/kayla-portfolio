Planning & Scheduling

Keeping third-shift POD moving when the queue is a mess

Every third shift starts with a queue of roughly 115 to 350 jobs that need sorting before the first sheet hits the feeder. Some have missing files, wrong stock, or bad ticket specs that would kill output — or worse, reach bindery already wrong.

Featured case Queue sequencing ERP discipline High-mix POD

Sequencing and exception management in third-shift POD

Protecting output when due dates are next-morning and the queue has gaps in it.

The real problem

POD jobs on third shift often have next-morning due dates. A missing file, a stock substitution that wasn't caught, or a ticket with wrong specs doesn't just stall the press — it backs up cylinder, stitching, and cutting too if it gets through without being flagged first.

What I actually do

I sequence the queue early — before the presses are warmed up — by due date, what bindery needs first, which press each job actually runs better on, whether the stock is physically on hand, and whether the PDF is imposed correctly for production. Calibration time is when I verify files and stage carts, not improvise mid-run.

Cross-department connections

  • Check job readiness against PACE and POD records before printing, not after.
  • Send one consolidated email at shift end: what ran, what's held, what needs morning follow-up, and who owns the fix.
  • Keep a physical basket near the scheduler's area for anything that needs a decision the next shift can't make alone.
  • Stamp and label anything going downstream with discrepancy info so bindery and cylinder aren't guessing.

What it adds up to

Beginning-of-shift prep saves roughly 10–15 minutes of preventable downtime per shift. Fewer errors reach bindery and cylinder. The morning shift has something organized to pick up. What's currently informal should eventually live in ERP with defined exception categories and named owners.

SOP case On-time delivery Risk control

Protecting 98% on-time POD performance with staging standard work

After a misplaced unfinished POD set nearly triggered a financial penalty, I treated it as a system failure and wrote SOP IND-001 within 24 hours.

What happened

An unfinished POD job jacket for a multi-file set lost visibility after being left without clear labeling or ownership, creating real on-time delivery and financial penalty risk.

My response

Once the operational and financial risk was made clear, I decided informal staging habits were no longer acceptable for high-priority POD work. Drafted and submitted SOP IND-001 within 24 hours.

What the SOP defined

  • Dedicated skid location near the Indigo 100K pile feeder.
  • Visual labels with job ticket number, customer, operator, target shift/date, and do-not-move warning.
  • Movement authority linked to end-of-shift reporting for auditable handoff visibility.

The pattern

IND-001 is one example of an ongoing practice. I write SOPs as incidents happen, propose them to the operations manager, and pass the practices along to other operators on the floor. The goal is always waste reduction and penalty avoidance.
