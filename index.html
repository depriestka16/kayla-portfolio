Case study 1

Job sequencing and exception triage in high-mix POD

Protected throughput on a third-shift POD queue by sequencing to downstream process needs, triaging ticket/file/stock issues, and keeping the line moving instead of stopping for every discrepancy.

Throughput

Business problem and risk

Third shift carries time-sensitive POD work with tight next-morning requirements while dealing with missing stock, missing files, and ticket mismatches that can disrupt cylinder, stitching, folding, and cutting if not handled quickly.

My role and decisions

I sequence by downstream process path, due date, account grouping, press strengths, and stock availability. During press calibration, I verify files and stock, pre-stage carts, and prepare feeders so changeovers stay controlled. Safe ticket discrepancies are corrected at the press; blocked work is moved into a defined holding path rather than consuming the whole shift.

Cross-functional actions

  • Used PACE and POD/MRP workflows to verify readiness before release.
  • Sent consolidated end-of-shift emails to the POD system owner, scheduler, CSRs, and prepress based on issue type and urgency.
  • Created a dedicated basket for scheduler follow-up and coordinated directly with bindery and cylinder on overs, handoff status, and rerouting.
  • Color-coded and stamped discrepancies to speed the next process instead of relying on handwritten notes.

Result / impact

Beginning-of-shift staging and structured triage save roughly 10–15 minutes per shift in preventable downtime, compounding to about 15 hours per month of recovered productive time in normal conditions. With available files and stock, approximately 99% of jobs still move forward because the system is built to adapt around minor errors rather than stop for them.

Standard work opportunity

This informal control system should become formal ERP/POD standard work: exception categories, ownership routing, overs defaults, and clearer queue logic tied to real downstream needs.

Case study 2

Protecting 98% on-time POD performance with staging standard work

After a misplaced unfinished POD set nearly triggered a financial penalty, created SOP IND-001 to lock down staging, labeling, movement, and ownership across shifts.

On-time delivery

Business problem and risk

An unfinished POD job jacket for a multi-file set was left on a light table without labeling or ownership, then moved, temporarily losing visibility. Leadership later explained that the near miss almost pushed POD performance below the 98% on-time threshold tied to financial penalties.

My role and decisions

I treated the incident as a system failure, not a one-off mistake. Once the operational and financial risk was made clear in a face-to-face discussion with the operations manager, I decided informal staging habits were no longer acceptable for high-priority POD work.

Actions taken

  • Drafted and submitted SOP IND-001 within 24 hours of the incident.
  • Defined POD work as high-priority and assigned one dedicated skid location near the Indigo 100K pile feeder.
  • Required visual labels with job ticket number, customer name, operator name, target completion shift/date, and “HIGH PRIORITY – DO NOT MOVE.”
  • Restricted movement authority and required email notification, written notes, log updates, and reasons for any relocation.
  • Linked end-of-shift reporting to staged-work visibility so handoffs could be audited and tracked.

Result / impact

The SOP removed ambiguous staging behavior and created a default operating path for incomplete POD sets. It reduced the risk of misplaced work, improved accountability across shifts, and connected floor behavior directly to on-time delivery protection and margin preservation.

Standard work opportunity

The next step would be linking high-priority status, owner, and location inside ERP/POD workflows so the physical rule and the system rule match.

Case study 3

Stepping into offset under pressure and driving record-level output

Volunteered as interim second pressman on a Komori Lithrone G40P to onboard a new hire, then helped the crew deliver 70,000 net sheets in one shift and earn company recognition.

Capacity

Business problem and risk

The company needed experienced support on a Komori Lithrone G40P during a critical period, and poor onboarding or weak press support would have hurt production, crew confidence, and shift output.

My role and decisions

I volunteered to step in as interim second pressman because I already had prior Komori exposure and could contribute immediately. I approached the role with a throughput mindset: support the press crew, help stabilize execution, and remove avoidable drag during a high-pressure period.

Actions taken

  • Supported onboarding and real-time learning for the new hire while maintaining production pace.
  • Helped the crew execute disciplined make-ready and run strategy instead of chasing rated speed blindly.
  • Worked within real stock, setup, and press constraints rather than theoretical max-speed assumptions.

Result / impact

Within less than a week on the press, the crew achieved the second-best production record on that machine: 70,000 net saved sheets in one 8-hour shift, earning recognition through the company recognition system. That result matters because the Komori may be rated up to 14,000 sheets per hour, but real-world throughput depends on stock, makeready, changeovers, and crew coordination.

Standard work opportunity

The lesson is not “run at nameplate speed.” It is that strong onboarding, realistic constraints, and disciplined support around the press can move capacity meaningfully even in a challenging production environment.
