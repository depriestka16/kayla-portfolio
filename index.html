High-mix POD

Keeping third-shift POD moving when the queue is a mess

Every third shift starts with a queue of roughly 115 to 350 jobs that need sorting before the first sheet hits the feeder. Some have missing files, wrong stock, or bad ticket specs that would kill output—or worse, reach bindery already wrong. Here's how I manage that so the shift actually finishes.

The real problem

POD jobs on third shift often have next-morning due dates. A missing file, a stock substitution that wasn't caught, or a ticket with wrong specs doesn't just stall the press—it backs up cylinder, stitching, and cutting too if it gets through without being flagged first.

What I actually do

I sequence the queue early—before the presses are warmed up—by due date, what bindery needs first, which press each job actually runs better on, and whether the stock is physically on hand. Calibration time is when I verify files and stage the carts, not improvise mid-run.

How it connects to other departments

  • Check job readiness against PACE and POD records before printing, not after.
  • Send one consolidated email at shift end: what ran, what's held, what needs morning follow-up, and who owns the fix.
  • Keep a physical basket near the scheduler's area for anything that needs a decision the next shift can't make alone.
  • Stamp and label anything going downstream with discrepancy info so bindery and cylinder aren't guessing.

What it adds up to

The beginning-of-shift prep saves roughly 10–15 minutes of preventable downtime per shift. The bigger value is that fewer errors reach bindery and cylinder, and the morning shift has something organized to pick up rather than a pile of unknowns. What's currently informal should eventually live in ERP with defined exception categories and named owners.

On-time delivery SOP ownership
Risk control

Protecting 98% on-time POD performance with staging standard work

After a misplaced unfinished POD set nearly triggered a financial penalty, I treated the event as a system failure and wrote SOP IND-001 to tighten staging, labeling, movement, and ownership across shifts.

Business problem

An unfinished POD job jacket for a multi-file set lost visibility after being left without clear labeling or ownership, creating real on-time delivery risk.

My response

Once the operational and financial risk was made clear, I decided informal staging habits were no longer acceptable for high-priority POD work.

Actions taken

  • Drafted and submitted SOP IND-001 within 24 hours.
  • Defined a dedicated skid location near the Indigo 100K pile feeder.
  • Required visual labels with job ticket number, customer name, operator, target shift/date, and a do-not-move warning.
  • Linked movement authority and end-of-shift reporting to auditable handoff visibility.

Result and next step

The SOP removed ambiguity, improved accountability, and tied floor behavior directly to on-time delivery protection. The logical next move is to link priority status, owner, and location inside ERP / POD workflows so the physical rule and system rule match.

Komori Lithrone G40P

Stepping into offset under pressure and supporting record-level output

I volunteered as interim second pressman support on a Komori Lithrone G40P during a critical period, helped onboard a new hire, and contributed to a 70,000-net-sheet shift through disciplined support rather than inflated speed claims.

Business problem

The company needed experienced support on the Komori during a critical period. Weak onboarding or poor support would have hurt production pace, crew confidence, and usable output.

My role

I stepped in because I had prior Komori exposure and could contribute quickly. My focus was to remove avoidable drag and stabilize the crew under pressure.

Actions taken

  • Supported onboarding and real-time learning for the new hire.
  • Helped the crew execute disciplined make-ready and run strategy.
  • Worked within real stock, setup, and press constraints instead of chasing nameplate speed.

Result and lesson

Within less than a week on the press, the crew achieved the second-best production record on that machine: 70,000 net saved sheets in one shift. The lesson is that realistic constraints, strong onboarding, and disciplined crew support move capacity more than heroic assumptions.
